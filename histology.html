Before pathology begins, we must first understand what "normal" looks like. In a typical blood smear:
  • RBCs appear pink with central pallor (acidophilic, biconcave, anucleate).
  • Neutrophils are larger, with segmented nuclei and granular cytoplasm.
  • Lymphocytes are small with high N:C ratio and dense chromatin.
  • Platelets are tiny cell fragments involved in clotting.

Special stains help differentiate immature leukemic cells:
  • Romanowsky stains highlight nuclear and cytoplasmic details; lymphoblasts appear large, with prominent nucleoli and a deep basophilic cytoplasm.
  • Diff-Quik, a rapid stain, brings out fine chromatin patterns and nuclear irregularities.

The hallmark of ALL: an overabundance of immature lymphoblasts. These large cells:
  • Have a high N:C ratio
  • Show fine chromatin and prominent nucleoli
  • Lack granules
Found in both peripheral blood and marrow.
