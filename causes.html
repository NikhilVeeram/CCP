Genetic Mutations and Chromosomal Abnormalities
  • Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)) creates a BCR-ABL fusion gene seen in some ALL cases.
  • Other translocations (like t(12;21)) can interfere with normal blood cell development.
  • Inherited mutations in genes like PAX5 or TP53 increase susceptibility.
  • Somatic mutations in lymphoid progenitors can trigger uncontrolled proliferation.

Source: Frontiers in Public Health

Environmental and Chemical Exposures
  • High doses of ionizing radiation (e.g., radiation therapy, atomic exposure) increase ALL risk.
  • Long-term exposure to benzene (found in gasoline, tobacco smoke) is a known carcinogen.
  • Occupational exposure in rubber or chemical industries may be linked to leukemia development.
  • Residential proximity to nuclear facilities has been loosely associated with pediatric ALL.

Source: IARC

Immune System and Infections in Early Childhood
  • Lack of early childhood exposure to common microbes may alter immune development ("delayed infection" hypothesis).
  • Infections like EBV or HTLV-1 have been linked to rare leukemia forms.
  • Dysregulated immune responses may create inflammatory environments that facilitate leukemogenesis.
  • ALL incidence sometimes spikes after viral outbreaks in children, though evidence remains inconclusive.

Source: Biomedicines (MDPI) and Frontiers in Immunology

Prenatal and Developmental Risk Factors
  • Some ALL mutations are detectable in utero, suggesting a prenatal origin of disease.
  • Maternal exposure to toxins, pesticides, or high radiation can affect fetal hematopoiesis.
  • Low birth weight and premature delivery may correlate with increased ALL risk in infants.
  • Advanced parental age, especially paternal, has been weakly linked to higher leukemia incidence.

Source: PMC - Developmental Origins of ALL
