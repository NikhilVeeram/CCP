Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) is an aggressive cancer of the blood-forming tissues. It causes immature lymphoid cells ‚Äî called lymphoblasts ‚Äî to multiply uncontrollably, flooding the blood and bone marrow. The disease progresses quickly and requires urgent treatment.

Subtypes include:

  • B-cell ALL: 75‚Äì80% of cases
  • T-cell ALL: Often more aggressive
  • Philadelphia Chromosome-positive ALL: Affects prognosis and treatment

Source: St. Jude Children's Research Hospital

ü©∫ Signs and Symptoms

ALL symptoms emerge rapidly. Many are due to the bone marrow being overrun and unable to produce normal blood cells:

  • Fatigue and pallor (low RBCs)
  • Bruising and bleeding (low platelets)
  • Frequent infections (low WBCs)
  • Bone pain, especially in long bones
  • Fever, night sweats, weight loss
  • Swollen lymph nodes, liver, or spleen

Source: T.L.D.R. Pharmacy - Introduction to Pediatric Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Factors
  • Genetic Syndromes: Down, Li-Fraumeni, Wiskott-Aldrich
  • Radiation Exposure: in utero or during cancer therapy
  • Ethnicity and Sex: More common in white males
  • Age: Older children (>1 year) or adults (>50)
  • Robertsonian Translocation: Presence of the Philadelphia chromosome
Genetic trigger: The Philadelphia chromosome forms a BCR-ABL fusion gene that promotes relentless cell division. (Penn Medicine)
